Right to Repair for Medical Equipment and Wheelchairs

Right to repair would enable hospitals and other medical centers to get their equipment repaired faster and cheaper. This could help make them more resistant to large surges in patients, such as those seen during the COVID-19 pandemic and would lower the overall cost of healthcare, for both consumers and insurance programs. (Some of which are publicly funded.) Below is an brief interview by Louis Rossman (a right to repair activist) with representatives of Mobile Instrument, a company that repairs surgical equipment.

One particularly notable piece of medical equipment are wheelchairs. If a wheelchair is broken, it has the potential to effectively immobilize its owner until it is repaired. But, as with most industries involving tech, manufacturers don't typically provide options for users to repair their wheelchair on their own or by using an independant repair provider. In addition, insurance companies will often specify specific manufacturers who they will pay to repair a wheelchair. In a study conducted by the PIRG Education Fund, 62% of respondants indicated that they, on average, had to wait four or more weeks for a wheelchair repair (page 18).
